    A Professional Life

    Banksy began his career as a freehand graffiti artist in 1992-1994, alongside Kato and Tes. He had moved to the skill of stenciling by the year 2000, after noticing that it took him less time to complete any of his art work. At 2003, Banksy held a show called “Turf War” in a warehouse where he painted animals. Within a year, he continued to create subversive paintings, such as Monet’s Water Lily Pond, which was altered to contain urban debris like rubbish. In 2004, he is said to have printed roughly 1 million £10 notes featuring Princess Diana’s head instead of the Queen’s. He also modified “Bank of England” to “Banksy of England” in the text. Since then, these notes have been selling for around £200 each on eBay. In 2006, two of his works were auctioned for a considerable fortune of £25000 and £50,400, respectively, breaking an auction record. Many other pieces of his art have drew attention to edgy subjects and the audacity with which they were executed. One example is the Israeli-Palestinian barrier in the West Bank, which gained a lot of media attention in 2005. Because of the soaring pricing of Banksy’s street art, a journalist named Max Foster coined the term “The Banksy Effect.” His worldwide fame has elevated his work from vandalism to collectible high art. Last but not least, his art continues to transport him throughout the globe. Other countries, such as the United States, Canada, Israel, Jamaica, Italy, Australia, and France, have enabled art to flourish and protected it.
